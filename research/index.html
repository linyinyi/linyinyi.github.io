Research

Four connected research themes — GeoAI and multisource remote sensing, urban environmental health, climate risk and community resilience, and sustainable urbanization.

My research program asks a single connected question: how do urbanization, climate extremes, and environmental inequality shape human health, community resilience, and sustainable development — and how can satellite observation and geospatial artificial intelligence answer that question at the scales where decisions are made?

The four themes below build on one another. Methods developed under GeoAI and multisource remote sensing supply the exposure and land-change evidence used in the three applied themes.

GeoAI and multisource remote sensing

I develop geospatial artificial intelligence methods that fuse optical, SAR, LiDAR, hyperspectral, and nighttime-light observations into consistent, decision-ready maps. This work combines machine learning and deep learning with physical understanding of the urban environment, and scales through cloud platforms such as Google Earth Engine.

Key questions

  • How can multisource satellite observations be fused when clouds, shadows, and sensor differences make any single source unreliable?
  • How do we make deep-learning-based mapping transparent enough to support environmental policy?

Methods. Deep learning and machine learning, data fusion, sparse representation, fuzzy-set approaches, Google Earth Engine, spatially explicit modelling

Scale. City to global; annual to intra-year time series

Urban environmental health

Cities concentrate people and environmental hazards. I quantify urban heat, air pollution, and their compound exposure at the scales where people actually live — buildings, neighbourhoods, and communities — and link them to mortality risk, health inequalities, and the social determinants that make some populations, including older adults, more vulnerable than others.

Key questions

  • How much larger are health risks when urban heat islands and air-pollution islands compound, and who bears them?
  • Which communities face the greatest combined environmental burden, and why?

Methods. Satellite-derived exposure mapping, structural equation modelling, multiscale spatial analysis, health-registry linkage

Scale. Building and community scale to global city samples

Climate risk and community resilience

Floods, droughts, and extreme temperatures increasingly arrive together. I map where compound and cascading climate risks concentrate, and use satellite evidence — from disaster-impact records to monthly nighttime lights — to measure how communities are disturbed by shocks and how quickly they recover, with attention to climate vulnerability and environmental justice.

Key questions

  • Where are communities least able to absorb compound floods, droughts, and heat extremes?
  • Can longitudinal satellite observations reveal resilience — disturbance and recovery — directly?

Methods. Disaster-impact spatialization, random forest, fuzzy-logic integration, time-series analysis of nighttime lights, high-resolution risk assessment

Scale. Community to global (multi-kilometre grids); monthly to multi-decadal

Sustainable urbanization

Understanding how cities expand — and at what environmental and social cost — requires long, consistent observation. I map urban land-cover change and expansion from optical and SAR time series, monitor urban growth across regions, and connect these patterns to urban–rural inequalities and the policy questions of sustainable, resilient cities.

Key questions

  • How do urban expansion and renewal unfold across decades, and what do they displace?
  • Which spatial evidence do policymakers need to steer urbanization toward sustainability?

Methods. Optical–SAR time-series analysis, impervious-surface mapping, change detection, urban growth monitoring

Scale. Neighbourhood renewal to global expansion; 1990s to present

Research vision

My long-term goal is to build high-resolution, explainable, and socially meaningful geospatial evidence — evidence that helps cities and communities adapt to climate extremes, protect public health, and grow more sustainably.

Getting there requires three things at once: observation systems that see every city and settlement consistently; models whose outputs scientists and policymakers can interrogate rather than take on faith; and research questions set by what matters for people — health, safety, and fair access to a livable environment. Each project I take on is chosen to advance at least one of these fronts.
